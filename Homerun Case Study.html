About the Company

The homeowner conversion problem and the contractor retention problem share one structural failure: the app is designed around products, not around the problems people are trying to solve. Homeowners do not think in SKUs and Contractors do not think in product pages. Every solution in this document begins with that observation and works backward to the product change.

Measurement anxiety kills purchase intent faster than price does
Finding 02
Contractor loyalty to offline suppliers is about consequence absorption, not cost

Section 02

Redesigning the Homeowner Experience

Insights from Field Research
#InsightDescription
01 The SKU-Project Mismatch Homeowners do not shop for products. They shop for solutions to spatial problems. "My kitchen floor is 120 sq. ft and I want it to look premium but not cold" is the real query. Any experience starting with a product category, not a project or room, is starting in the wrong place.
02 Uncertainty Cost Dominates Price Sensitivity In early-stage purchasing, homeowners are not concerned about price. They are concerned about making a mistake they cannot reverse. The perceived cost of uncertainty exceeds the cost of paying 10–15% more at a local shop.
03 Measurement Paralysis is a Solvable UX Problem Every homeowner in field observation knew the room they wanted to tile but could not translate a room into an order quantity. "How many boxes do I need?" killed conversion intent in every session. This is not a customer education problem; it is a calculator-as-product problem.
04 The Renovation Has Chapters No homeowner renovates their entire home at once. They move through a project in phases: floor first, then walls, then fixtures, then paint. Each phase has a 2–6-week gap. Homerun's M1 retention problem is partly explained by having no product mechanism that acknowledges this chapter structure.
05 The "Delivery Promise vs. Reality" Gap is a Trust Architecture Failure The "120 Mins" badge is directly contradicted by "Your order will get processed at 8 AM on the next day" for evening orders. A user building a ₹50,000 cart at 7:30 PM expects 2-hour delivery. They hit this message after 20 minutes of effort. They do not come back.
5 High-Impact Improvements
01
Time-Honest Delivery Promise
Stage: Discovery → Trust · Addresses Critical #1 from audit
Customer Insight
The '120 Mins' badge makes a promise that is factually wrong for all evening orders. Users discover the contradiction after building their cart — a trust-destruction event that drives session abandonment and kills return intent. This is Homerun's most acute trust failure, visible on every home screen screenshot.
The Change
The delivery badge becomes dynamic based on time of day. Before cutoff: 'Delivery by 4:30 PM.' After cutoff: 'First slot: Tomorrow 8–10 AM.' A delivery slot selector appears at the TOP of the home screen, not at checkout. For after-hours users, the cart persists overnight with a WhatsApp reminder at 7:30 AM: 'Your 8 AM delivery is confirmed. Materials are being picked.'
Expected Impact
Evening session (6–10 PM) cart completion rate is projected to increase. This fix requires only backend logic change and no new UI components. Highest confidence, lowest effort of all five improvements.
Validation Metric
Evening session cart completion rate. Customer care contacts per order attributable to delivery confusion. This is the single most important quick win.
02
Project Cost Estimator on PDP
Stage: PDP → Cart → AOV · Addresses High friction at Pricing stage
Customer Insight
"Can I afford to tile my kitchen with this?" is the real conversion question. The current PDP answers '₹85 per sq. ft' — meaningless without knowing how many sq. ft are needed, what accessories cost and what delivery adds up to. Measurement paralysis kills intent before cart add.
The Change
Below product images on the tile PDP, add a 'Calculate for my project' module. User inputs room size (with a visual size guide for those without a measuring tape: 'medium kitchen ≈ 120 sq. ft'). Instantly shows: Tiles needed + 20% wastage allowance, recommended accessories (adhesive, grout) in correct quantities, delivery cost, and a total project cost. A single 'Add everything to cart — ₹13,830' CTA.
Expected Impact
Higher PDP → Cart conversion. Increase AOV via accessory bundling. The ₹10,000 free delivery threshold becomes a natural pull mechanic — most project-kit orders clear it, eliminating the checkout sticker shock.
Validation Metric
PDP → Cart conversion: estimator users vs non-users. Average items per cart. % of carts clearing ₹10,000 free delivery threshold. AOV delta: estimator sessions vs standard sessions.
03
Project-Mode Onboarding
Stage: Discovery → Browse → Activation
Customer Insight
Homeowners arrive at the app with an outcome ('I want a nice kitchen floor'), not a product ('600mm vitrified tile'). The current home screen — a category grid of Cement, Tiling, Plywood — is designed for someone who already knows what they need. The customer type modal exists but is dismissible with a single X tap.
The Change
On first app open, replace the category grid with a full-screen prompt: 'What are you working on?' with 6 visual tiles (Kitchen Renovation, Bathroom, Full Home, Flooring Only, Painting, Other). Each tile shows a finished room, not a product. The modal is non-skippable on first launch; power users have a 'I know what I need → Browse directly' escape path.
Expected Impact
Increase in install-to-first-search conversion. Users with a saved project have higher M1 retention — this is the downstream effect that makes project-mode the highest LTV investment in the list, even though it ranks #3 on immediate conversion impact.
Validation Metric
% of new users completing project setup in M0. Install → First Cart Add rate: project-mode cohort vs direct browse. M1 retention by project-mode cohort vs control. Time to first meaningful action (sub-3-minute benchmark).
04
Delivery Architecture: Slot Selection + Inside-Flat Option
Stage: Checkout → Delivery → NPS
Customer Insight
Construction materials are not like grocery orders. A homeowner ordering 150 tiles needs someone home, may need a helper to carry them upstairs, and needs their contractor scheduled to start the next day. Vague delivery windows make all of this impossible to plan.
The Change
At checkout, show a delivery window selector: 9–11 AM, 11 AM–1 PM, 2–4 PM, 4–6 PM. For heavy orders (>100 Kg), surface 'Inside flat delivery: 2 helpers, ₹149' proactively on the PDP. Order confirmation via WhatsApp (not only app push) with exact window and live tracking link. If any item ships separately, notify proactively before delivery, not after.
Expected Impact
Decrease in post-order customer care contacts. Increased post-first-order NPS. The delivery experience is the last thing a homeowner remembers before deciding whether to return.
Validation Metric
Customer care contacts per order (decline). Delivery NPS (post-delivery survey). Same-delivery cancellation rate. M1 retention: delivery-slot-selected cohort vs unscheduled cohort.
05
Renovation Milestone Nudge Engine
Stage: Post-Purchase → O1 → O2 Repeat
Customer Insight
After buying kitchen floor tiles, the homeowner does not open Homerun again for 3 weeks while the contractor installs. By the time they are ready for Chapter 2 (wall tiles), they have defaulted to a local hardware shop. Homerun's M1 retention problem is a timing problem, not a product problem.
The Change
After a floor-tile order, start a 14-day timer (average installation duration). At Day 14, send a WhatsApp message: 'Most kitchens take 1–2 weeks to install new flooring. If your floor is done, here's what homeowners who ordered this tile bought next.' In-app, a persistent renovation tracker shows progress: Kitchen Floor ✓ → Walls (Next) → Fixtures → Paint.
Expected Impact
Increased M1 retention. Increased orders-per-project. A homeowner retained through a full kitchen renovation generates 4–5 orders over 10–12 weeks — worth ₹28,000–₹35,000 in incremental GMV at ₹7,000 AOV per order.
Validation Metric
M1 retention rate: 30-day repeat purchase. Orders per project lifecycle. Nudge open rate and conversion rate by message type. Time between O1 and O2 (shorter = better lifecycle capture).

OPD & M1 Retention Impact Summary

Taken together, the five improvements address the complete homeowner funnel from session start to repeat purchase. The delivery promise fix prevents trust destruction before any product is seen. The PDP estimator converts browsing intent into completed carts. The delivery slot feature converts post-purchase anxiety into NPS. The milestone engine is the only mechanism that turns a single transaction into a project-long relationship.

A homeowner retained through a full kitchen renovation generates 4–5 orders over 10–12 weeks. At ₹7,000 AOV per order and higher for bundled project kits, each retained homeowner represents ₹28,000–₹35,000 in incremental GMV that currently goes to local hardware shops. Win M1, and every subsequent renovation phase becomes a near-zero-CAC acquisition.

5 Critical Win Moments vs. the Hardware Supplier
1
Transparent Trade Pricing vs. Negotiation Theatre
Offline Reality
Contractor calls shop owner, gets a verbal price that may or may not be their real price. Negotiation is a social ritual — takes time, requires relationship investment, and the contractor never knows if they got the best deal.
What's Broken
Information asymmetry benefits the supplier. The contractor cannot compare prices across suppliers without multiple calls. Uncertainty in every transaction creates low-level anxiety.
Homerun Pro Solution
Transparent 3-tier pricing visible immediately on every SKU. Volume thresholds shown proactively. No negotiation required — the trade price is the trade price. The psychological value of 'I know I got the right price' is nearly as important as the price itself.
Switching Incentive
Predictability is the product. Once contractors trust that Homerun's price is consistently competitive, the cognitive overhead of shopping drops to near zero.
2
One-Tap Reorder vs. WhatsApp Back-and-Forth
Offline Reality
Contractor sends a WhatsApp message: 'Bhai, 10 bags OPC, 5 bags sand, 3 cartons tile (30×60 ivory gloss). Koramangala site. Kal subah 8 baje.' Shop owner sometimes misreads it. Materials sometimes arrive wrong.
What's Broken
Error rate in manual WhatsApp orders is significant. Wrong SKU, wrong quantity, wrong site cause project delays. No record of what was ordered without scrolling through chat history.
Homerun Pro Solution
Saved Lists + Reorder Last + confirmed order record. A contractor's daily order that takes 30 minutes of WhatsApp back-and-forth is replaced by a 45-second app interaction.
Switching Incentive
Time saved alone is compelling. But the deeper incentive is error elimination — a wrong material delivered to a site delays the entire project, which costs far more than any price difference.
3
GST-Compliant Digital Invoices vs. Handwritten Bills
Offline Reality
Hardware shop bills are frequently handwritten, often missing GSTIN or with incorrect HSN codes. At quarter-end, contractors spend 2–3 days chasing corrected bills so their accountant can file GST returns.
What's Broken
A contractor with ₹3 lakh/month in material purchases and correct GST documentation is leaving ₹54,000/month (18% input tax credit) on the table purely due to documentation failure.
Homerun Pro Solution
Every Homerun Pro order auto-generates a GST-compliant invoice with correct GSTIN, HSN code, and tax breakdown. 'Download Q4 invoices for CA' in one tap. This is not a feature; it is financial infrastructure.
Switching Incentive
The tax saving alone is ₹54,000/month for a ₹3L/month buyer — which exceeds any delivery premium Homerun charges. This is the single most quantifiable switching incentive in the Pro proposition.
4
Verified Stock Confirmation vs. 'Hoga Ya Nahi?'
Offline Reality
Contractors ask 'Will it be available?' every time they order large quantities. Shop owners often confirm availability without checking, leading to partial deliveries at 8 AM when a concrete pour is scheduled for 9 AM.
What's Broken
A partial delivery on a pour day means rescheduling an entire concrete pour — 8–12 workers paid ₹800–1,200/day who do nothing. One missed pour = ₹7,000–₹15,000 in wasted labour.
Homerun Pro Solution
For orders above 20 units of any SKU: 'Confirmed 200 bags in stock at HSR warehouse. Delivery tomorrow 8–10 AM.' If stock is insufficient, immediately surface alternatives with spec comparison.
Switching Incentive
Removing pour-day uncertainty is worth a significant premium to any contractor who has experienced it. Once they experience reliable stock confirmation, the informal verbal confirmation from a shop owner feels like an unnecessary gamble.
5
Pre-Approved Credit vs. Relationship-Dependent Lending
Offline Reality
Getting credit from a hardware shop requires a personal relationship. The credit limit is unstated, renegotiated implicitly and can be withdrawn at any time. The contractor is in a power-inferior position.
What's Broken
Informal credit is reliable until it is not. During peak demand (pre-festive surge, post-monsoon resumption), shop owners restrict credit to preferred customers.
Homerun Pro Solution
Pre-approved, transparent credit facility with a stated limit, clear repayment terms and no interpersonal power dynamic. Contractor knows exactly how much credit they have, when it is due and how to increase it.
Switching Incentive
This shifts the credit relationship from dependency to predictable business tool. The contractor does not need to manage a personal relationship to access working capital.

Phase 2 is built once order data exists to personalise (predictive reorder), repayment history exists to expand credit responsibly and GMV per contractor justifies dedicated account management.

Riskiest MVP assumption: GST auto-verification via GSTN API is reliable for the smaller contractor segment where GST registration may be informal or incomplete.
Test: Running 50 manual verifications in Week 1 alongside the API to calibrate failure rate and design the fallback flow before launch.

Section 05

Trade-Offs, Self-Critique & What I'd Do Differently

Explicit Trade-offs Made
Trade-off 1: Delivery promise fix shipped before project-mode onboarding
Project-mode onboarding is a larger surface change that carries implementation risk. The delivery promise fix is a 2-week backend logic change. Shipping in this order means the most acute trust failure is addressed immediately, while the larger UX restructure is validated against real conversion data before committing to it. The downside: homeowners who install during the 4–6-week gap still experience the category-first home screen.
Trade-off 2: Milestone engine ships before visual inspiration layer
A room-visualiser and AR preview would address the trust gap at the PDP stage. I ranked the milestone engine higher because retention compounds — each homeowner retained through a full renovation is worth 5–8× a single-order homeowner. The inspiration layer is a better user experience but delivers returns on a longer timeline and requires more operational investment (content curation, photo moderation).
Trade-off 3: Pro credit shipped as rule-based pre-approval, not full ML underwriting
Full ML-based credit scoring using GST returns and order history requires data Homerun does not yet have in volume. The rule-based pre-approval (GST turnover above threshold → ₹15,000 starter credit) ships in MVP and provides the contractor experience without the underwriting risk. The downside: it excludes smaller contractors with informal income not captured in GST. This is a deliberate decision to protect the credit default rate guardrail while we build order history.
Self-Critique

On the Homeowner Side

  • The project-mode onboarding might add friction for the returning user. Someone who already knows they need OPC cement should have a clean 'Browse directly' escape that does not punish them. I under-specified this in the UX flow — it needs explicit design.
  • The cost estimator assumes users can provide a room dimension. Many will not have a measuring tape or dimensions in the initial stage. The UX needs a standard-size picker ('small / medium / large kitchen') as a fallback, and ideally a photo-based estimation tool. Without this, the estimator is useful only for the 40% of homeowners who measured before opening the app.
  • The milestone nudge engine could feel intrusive if timing is wrong. A nudge sent when the homeowner is still waiting for waterproofing to dry is irritating, not helpful. This requires real order-timing data to calibrate — it needs a pilot cohort before broad rollout, not a day-one launch.

On the Homerun Pro Side

  • The credit facility is the highest-value and highest-risk feature. GST API access is not always clean; smaller contractors have informal income not captured in GST. A bad credit call at scale creates NPA exposure. The underwriting model needs dedicated risk team input before expanding beyond the rule-based starter limit. Partnering with NBFCs would be a better call.
  • The material substitution engine in Phase 2 is technically complex and operationally risky. Suggesting a wrong substitute for a structural application (incorrect cement grade for a load-bearing pour) could cause real harm. Implementation must be conservative: automated for accessories, manual review for structural materials.
  • The sales motion for contractor acquisition is under-addressed. The Pro sign-up flow assumes a contractor who has already decided to try Homerun. The bigger challenge is reaching that moment of intent. Field sales + WhatsApp broadcast campaigns in target markets are a prerequisite to the Pro launch — the best product experience cannot compensate for weak go-to-market.
What I'd Do Differently with Access to Homerun's Internal Data

My recommendations are grounded in behavioural observation, app analysis and competitive research — but not in Homerun's actual funnel data. Before finalising any prioritisation, I would need:

  • The actual conversion rate at each funnel stage (install → first cart add → checkout → payment), to confirm whether my severity rankings match quantitative reality.
  • The percentage of orders placed in evening slots that currently abandon, to size the revenue opportunity of the delivery promise fix.
  • The distribution of cart sizes — specifically what percentage of Pro orders currently exceed the weight limit — to quantify the weight-split fix's GMV impact.
  • M1 retention rate by acquisition channel (organic, paid, referral), to understand whether the milestone engine is solving a discovery problem or a product-value problem.
  • Contractor order frequency and repeat patterns, to calibrate the predictive reorder timing in Phase 2.

Two interlinked systems that together shift Homerun from a catalog-led marketplace to a problem-solving layer — where offline suppliers currently dominate.

Flow 1 · Project Checkout Engine
From “I want to renovate” → Checkout in 4 taps | Project-Based Commerce UX
A project-first entry point that collapses search, quantity estimation, accessory discovery, and cost calculation into a single guided decision.
Screen 1
Project entry
Kitchen · Bathroom · Flooring — no SKUs, no categories. Users arrive with outcome intent, not product intent.
Screen 2
Scope selection
Small / Medium / Large with contextual guidance ("Most Indian kitchens are 100–150 sq ft"). Replaces measurement anxiety with confidence.
Screen 3 — Core moment
Auto-generated kit
System generates tiles, adhesive, grout, and wastage buffer — with total cost. Four decisions collapsed into one.
Screen 4
Customize & checkout
Edit quantities, swap products, real-time price updates. Flexibility without breaking the system.
Design principle
Users don't come here to buy tiles — they come to solve a space problem. This flow eliminates search, quantity estimation, accessory discovery, and cost calculation and replaces them with a single guided decision. Control is optional — confidence is default.
02
Flow 2 · Renovation OS
From Single Order → Full Renovation Journey | Lifecycle UX for Homeowners
The retention layer — turns a transaction into a timeline and drives repeat orders through smart, time-aware re-engagement.
Screen 1
Post-order transformation
First purchase auto-creates a "Kitchen Renovation" project. Flooring marked complete. We are turning a transaction into a timeline.
Screen 2
Project dashboard
Floor ✓ · Walls → Next · Fixtures · Paint. Gives users the mental model of what comes next — something offline shops do intuitively.
Screen 3
Smart re-engagement
Time + behaviour triggered modal: "Your flooring should be done by now." Timing is the real retention lever, not notifications.
Screen 4
Next-step kit
Pre-filled bundle based on previous order. The same decision engine drives O2, O3, O4 — reuse without re-friction.
Design principle
We don't wait for the user to come back — we re-enter at the right moment. The dashboard gives users a mental model of what comes next. The same bundle engine that won the first order drives every order that follows.
Strategic synthesis
Together, these two systems do different jobs
Flow 1 · Project Checkout Engine

Wins the first order by removing thinking. Search, estimation, accessory discovery, and cost calculation collapse into a single guided decision.

Flow 2 · Renovation OS

Wins the next 3–4 orders by owning the project timeline. Smart re-engagement at the right moment, not just at the next session.

Most solutions improve conversion. This approach captures the entire project lifecycle — effectively shifting Homerun from a catalog-led marketplace to a problem-solving layer, which is where offline suppliers currently dominate.

The best product thinking combines behavioural insight with data specificity. Field research is necessary but not sufficient.
